Abstract


Due to the scalability and editability, Scalable Vector Graphic (SVG) animation is widely used for applications ranging from web development to interactive design. However, SVG animations typically involve specifying transformations (e.g., translate, rotate, scale) for each element in the animation. This requires manually adjusting these properties for every frame or key moment in the animation, which can be time-consuming, especially for complex sequences. In this paper, we propose VecVideo, a text-guided method for generating SVG animations. VecVideo decomposes the animation generation process into two tasks: motion prediction and color prediction. For motion, VecVideo first samples a set of sparse control points and predicts their motion. Second, the motion of other points is propagated based on the predicted motion of the sparse control points. Third, to ensure motion coherence, a cross-frame consistency loss is applied to the motion of paths (which consist of multiple points). For color, VecVideo predicts the changes in path colors over time. After the two steps, a pre-trained text-to-video diffusion model is used to guide the motion and color prediction. Extensive experiments demonstrate the effectiveness and efficiency of our method, including qualitative and quantitative evaluations. We also showcase a few potential applications of VecVideo. The code has been submitted as supplementary material and will be made publicly available.

Method


VecVideo generates text-guided SVG animations by dividing the process into two stages: motion prediction and color prediction. First, motion is predicted for a subset of control points and propagated to others, with cross-frame consistency ensuring smooth transitions. Then, color attributes are adjusted to enhance realism. A pre-trained text-to-video diffusion model guides both stages, ensuring alignment with the textual prompt.

Limitations


Failure Cases

  • Difficulty in learning motion trajectories The model sometimes fails to produce coherent movement, resulting in unnatural animations.
  • Distortion of original shapes The structure of objects in SVGs can be deformed during the generation process.
  • Inconsistency across frames Visual inconsistencies such as flickering or abrupt transitions may occur between consecutive frames.

Possible Causes

These issues largely stem from the limitations of the current text-to-video (T2V) base model, which lacks specialized mechanisms for vector animation. In particular, the model struggles with preserving temporal coherence and shape fidelity in the SVG domain.

Future Work

We plan to explore model enhancements that improve frame-to-frame consistency and better preserve object geometry. This may include incorporating vector-specific priors and motion-aware design tailored for SVG animations.
